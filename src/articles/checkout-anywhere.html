About

With long-standing, successful and complex digital products, deciding whether to prioritise fixing problems or building new features can be a challenge. Often the decision comes down to whether it's better to fix what's there already or to risk starting again and building from scratch.

This case study looks at this challenge for a major online retailer and how I developed solutions using a lean product and UX approach.

User problems

I used quantative analytics data to investigate how customers were using the basket and checkout, identifying areas where people were having problems and dropping out of the flow. From this, I conducted a series of qualitative user interviews to dig further into the issues. The feedback I received fell into three main groups:

Work-arounds

While the checkout had a decent conversion rate, we could see from analytics data that there was a significant drop-out at basket page. Users would add a product to their basket, go back to the catalogue to look for more products, add them to their basket, and so on.

Research found that these users were using the basket as a comparison tool or to save products for later as this functionality wasn't available in the product catalogue.

Delivery surprises

We found there was a big customer drop-out between the checkout delivery and payment pages. Users would enter their delivery details and then either exit the checkout entirely or go back to the product catalogue and look for a different product.

Research found that users were entering the checkout as that was the only place where they could see the delivery times and cost. If the cost was too high or the delivery was inconvenient, they'd either try a different product or abandon the purchase entirely.

The 'Ryanair' effect

After adding a product to the basket, the user is asked to select add-on services, such as unwrapping, rubbish disposal, installation and recycling.

Users felt that while these services were worthwhile as they, along with the delivery fee, could add significantly to the price – an unwelcome surprise as this was not made clear before reaching the basket. One participant compared it to Ryanair, adding extra charges in the checkout and not being up-front about the real, higher price.

Product & technical problems

Not scalable

Complex

Difficult to add new user journeys that span multiple teams due to mis-aligned priorities & targets.

Hard to optimise

Teams can't take responsibility for their own areas or release independently.

Developers spend a lot of time doing things they shouldn’t have to, e.g. updating copy and adding services.

Slow pace

It takes a long time to do anything due to code complexity and duplication.

Can't improve UX

Fragile & inflexible tech means we can’t run tests or experiment with new flows and experiences.

UX review

To get a full picture of the problems and opportunities and to ensure everyone's ideas and suggestions were gathered, I held a series of UX review workshops with the product and developer squads, stakeholders and related teams. I synthesised all the research and data we had, including user insights, analytics, support queries and financial performance and added it to an end-to-end visualisation of the checkout journey.

Participants were invited to add 'how might we' statements to highlight problems or suggest new features.

Hypotheses mapping and prioritisation

Working as part of a product trio with the product owner and technical lead, we converted all the 'how might we' statements, problems and ideas from the UX mapping exercise into hypotheses that could be prioritised. We also worked through our existing backlog, prioritising items in the same way.

Eventually, after getting rid of a big chunk of the hypotheses, we were left with just the ones that we knew were worth persuing. While there was still a lot of work to be done, the backlogs for each squad could be much more manageable and we had an overriding theme to bring all the work together.

Journey improvements

We believe that by enabling customers to continue checking-out across devices or sessions, we will encourage customers to complete their purchase, reducing drop-off and increasing conversion.

We believe that by clearly showing the customer the decisions they've made, we will reduce the number of mistakes reported to the call centre, reinforce trust and increase confidence.

We believe that by decreasing perceived time to place an order, customers see us as being easy to use and therefore more likely to return.

We believe that providing entry to finance from the product page will enable a streamlined and more personal finance journey.

We believe that helping customers pay in the fastest, most convenient ways for them will reduce speed-to-purchase and increase confidence in themselves and our product.

We believe that improving accessibility will make the experience more effective for more people and improve brand trust and loyalty.

We believe that reducing common mistakes customers make while purchasing will reduce post-purchase support calls and customer frustration.

High value / high risk

User accounts

We believe that by better utilising data saved to customers’ accounts we can simplify their checkout experience, decreasing conversion time.

We believe that a personalised checkout will lead to a quicker, more tailored experience, making customers feel valued and therefore more likely to return.

We believe that by encouraging returning customers to sign in we can create a one-click shopping experience.

We believe that by making new users create an account we will encourage them to self-serve order issues.

Services & delivery

Customers weigh up delivery costs and availability as part of choosing a product, so we believe that accurate delivery information will help them feel confident that they have the full picture, with no hidden surprises.

We believe that making more of the additional benefits, such as services, will lead to an increased uptake and improve satisfaction. Selling a solution, not just a product.

People consider services as part of the total cost of a product, so we believe that the ability to add servcies at a product level will foster commitment to the purchase and improve quality of leads into basket.

Payments & promotions

We believe that enabling quick payment methods like PayPal or Apple Pay on relevant products will reduce payment friction and increase conversion.

We believe that incentivising referrals will drive sales and strengthen the brand. (People are more likely to trust brands recommended to them by friends and family).

Basket

We believe that by enabling the basket on every page, customers can get a persistent view of their order and begin checking out from anywhere.

Opportunity mapping

To further understand and refine the product plan we created an opportunity map, looking at where we wanted to end up and plotting out the steps of what we needed to learn, discover or build to get there.

Product vision

The process of putting together the opportunuty map helped us develop a vision of what we wanted the basket and checkout products to be – and what we needed to do to meet the needs and goals of the wider business.

From everything we'd learned from speaking to customers, listening to customer support calls, getting input from stakeholders and subject-matter experts, best practice, competitor research and hypotheses mapping, three themes emerged:

Personalised

Purchase journey is tailored to the customer - logged in journey, emergency vs premium purchase, finance etc.

Everywhere

Enable customer to find prices, add services, select timeslots and complate their purchase anywhere on the site.

Seamless

Quick, joined-up process from intent-to-buy to completed order. Faster logged in journeys, strong tech, multiple teams building one experience.

Customers have different reasons for buying an appliance, from the urgent need for clean socks to a more aspirational desire to get the latest technology. The checkout should be able to react to and support those needs.

Instead of forcing customers to go to the checkout, like being in a supermarket, customers should be able to purchase wherever and whenever they're ready, including from an off-site link such as a social media post.

Lean strategy

It was clear we had a lot of work to do

Salvage

Current strategy - fix or replace current systems piece-by-piece.

✅ Based on current systems, code & team knowledge.

✅ Lower risk as not building anything new.

❌ Code is old, fragile and may not be possible to fix.

❌ Slow – responsive checkout and basket liberation projects have been running for years.

Rewrite

Ground-up rewrite of basket and checkout using modern best practice.

✅ Fresh start, clean break between old and new.

❌ Risky – no guarantee replacement would perform better, danger of recreating current problems.

❌ Slow waterfall process, liable to feature-creep.

❌ Doesn’t deliver customer value while in development.

Disrupt

Launch with MVP, then build features in agile sprints. Retire legacy systems when enough functionality is complete.

✅ Lower risk as only building small pieces at a time.

✅ Rapid validation and feedback as systems will be used by customers from MVP release.

✅ Continually adds value by adding new features & functionality.

❌ Slow, but will add value throughout.

## LEAN approach ## Minimal valuable product ## Solution It was clear that something needed to change as the teams had reached a sort of stalemate. The platform was too complex and fragile to work on at any pace, but it wasn’t possible to properly get a handle on the technical issues as they were bigger than any one team. The business had been discussing for quite some time whether it was better to focus on salvaging the existing platform, or starting from scratch and building something new. These both had significant pros and cons\ ## Outcomes High staff turnover and competing priorities meant that we found it increasingly hard to get the resources and time we needed. Departures in the senior leadership team meant we also lost high-profile sponsors of the project. After some poor post-Covid financial results, the chief executive decided to launch a new company-wide initiative. All existing work was put on hold or cancelled to make room for it, including this project. ## Learning
